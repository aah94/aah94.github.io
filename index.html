Biography

I am a second-year Ph.D. student at Université Grenoble Alpes, supervised by Professor Massih-Reza Amini, and a member of the Aptikal team. I am actively exploring a diverse range of topics within machine learning and deep learning to advance scalable AI solutions through improved model efficiency. My main research focuses on efficient machine learning for vision models, leveraging techniques like tensor decomposition and knowledge distillation to compress large models. Additionally, I have been collaborating with the University of Basel for the past year on projects involving generative AI applied to imaging inverse problems, such as image denoising, MRI and CT.

All Efficient Deep Learning Inverse Problems

Model Compression

Complex neural networks, while highly accurate, require significant computational resources, making them challenging to deploy on resource-constrained devices such as mobile phones and embedded systems. To address this, compression algorithms have been developed to reduce model size and computational demands while preserving accuracy. This project presents a unified framework that combines decomposition with optimal rank selection to enhance efficiency. For further details, please refer to our paper.

Inverse Problem

Deep learning models for computational imaging often produce single-image estimates, which can be unreliable due to noise and model mismatch. Bayesian methods aim to approximate the posterior distribution for more accurate imaging, but conditional normalizing flows are limited by high memory and compute costs. This project, we introduces C-Trumpets, conditional injective flows tailored for imaging, which reduce memory use and training time while enhancing performance on tasks like limited-view CT and nonlinear inverse scattering, providing efficient point estimates and uncertainty quantification. For more information take a look at our paper.

Wave Scattering

Traditional inverse scattering solvers and emerging deep-learning methods often lack uncertainty measures, which can lead to inaccurate results in noisy, ill-posed conditions. In this project introduces U-Flow, a Bayesian U-Net model based on conditional normalizing flows, designed to generate high-quality posterior samples and physically-meaningful uncertainty estimates. U-Flow outperforms recent normalizing flow models in sample quality while matching U-Net in point estimation accuracy. Refer to our paper.
